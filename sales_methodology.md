# Exodus Property Solutions — Seller Call Sales Methodology

**Version 1.0 — Master Reference for AI Call Analysis**

This document is the single source of truth for how we run sales calls with motivated sellers. It is also the framework an AI analyst uses to grade recorded calls, identify pain points the seller revealed, and recommend specific tactics to move that seller toward contract.

The methodology is built on the Results Driven (Tiffany & Josh High) framework that Exodus has adopted, with Exodus-specific adaptations (Sell to Exodus branding, Florida market, Jo's leadership team).

---

## 1. Why This Exists — The Psychology

Every seller you talk to is conditioned to expect a salesperson. When their phone rings from a number they don't recognize, their immediate thought is "spam," "scam," or "someone trying to sell me something." We have **four seconds** to defeat that conditioned response — the warmth, energy, and confidence of the opening "Hey, this is [Name], how are you today?" is the first proof that we are not who they expected.

Real rapport is **not built through commonality** ("you have a dog, I have a dog"). It is built through **trust and credibility**. Trust is built by telling the seller exactly what is going to happen and then doing exactly that. Credibility is built by demonstrating competence — knowing the questions to ask, knowing the local market, having a verifiable identity (Facebook, website, address).

**Sellers will lie.** Sometimes the loneliness lies (an old woman bakes you chocolate chip cookies and tells you exactly what you want to hear because she wants the company). Sometimes the fear lies (they want to keep options open). Sometimes the strategy lies (they're shopping you against another offer). Our job is to verify, not to take statements at face value, and to spot when something they said early contradicts something they say later.

**Emotion drives decisions; logic backs them.** Every meaningful seller decision is made emotionally and rationalized after. If a seller is in a logical mind state, they are not ready to make a decision. Our job in the Process Call is to identify and amplify the emotional motivation, then carry them to the Offer Call in an emotional state. A human brain takes about 30 minutes to move from emotion back to logic — that is why we never let more than 30 minutes pass between the Process Call and the Offer Call.

**Resistance is wired in.** Humans push back against suggestions and demands by default, and the resistance intensifies under pressure, fear, or discomfort. Anything we do that increases those three increases resistance. Anything that lowers them lowers resistance. This is why we offer permission to say no — paradoxically, removing the pressure to say yes makes saying yes feel safe.

## 2. The Three-Call Architecture

Large-ticket items require multiple calls. Studies of 34,000 sales calls show that successful large-ticket sales contain four phases: preliminaries (intro + expectations), investigating questions (motivation, condition, roadblocks), demonstration of capability (the offer), and obtaining commitment.

We split these across **three calls** to build trust and credibility, and to give the seller's emotional state time to lock in:

1. **Process Call** — Investigation only. We discover the seller's situation, identify pain, run property condition diagnostics, surface roadblocks, set expectations for the Offer Call. **No price discussion.** Closes with: "I'm going to take 20–30 minutes to talk with my partners. They'll let me know what the property qualifies for. I'll call you back."

2. **Offer Call** — Demonstration of capability. We recap the seller's motivation back to them emotionally, walk them through what working with us looks like, present in-pocket math, then reveal the number. Closes with: "I'll take about 15 minutes to type the written offer. Then I'll call you back to walk you through it together — that takes about 5 minutes."

3. **Purchase Agreement Call** — Document review and signature. We walk the DocuSign together — name, address, price, walkthrough window, closing date, additional terms.

**Expectation Congruency Rule.** The end of each call must set the start expectations of the next call, and the next call must deliver exactly on those expectations. This is the mechanism that builds trust: we said it, then we did it.

## 3. The Qualification Gate — UMBC

A lead is **qualified** for the Process if any of these conditions are met:

- **Yes to Urgency + Distressed Motivation** → Process or Offer (this is the ideal path)
- **Yes to Urgency + Good Ballpark price** → Process (could lead to Offer)
- **No to Urgency, No Distress, Bad Price, but Not Livable** → Process (the property has to sell for cash because it can't be financed)

Otherwise → disqualify (politely, with a callback in 3–6 months).

**U — Urgency**: "If we come to an agreement on a fair price for your property, you are definitely ready to sell it, right?" Must get a clean YES. If no, ask why and try to overcome.

**M — Motivation**: Distressed or Not Distressed. If distressed, run the full Process. If not distressed, skip ahead and talk price immediately — don't waste time on Process discovery.

**B — Ballpark**: Compare to Zillow estimate (or our internal ARV). 90% or less of Zillow = qualified. 90% or more of Zillow = ask about condition.

**C — Condition**: Livable or Not Livable. If livable AND not motivated AND price too high → disqualify. If not livable → process (can't be financed, must sell for cash).

## 4. The Listening Lens — What to Hear in the Seller

Sellers are motivated by **moving away from pain** or **moving toward pleasure**. The picture-perfect seller is doing both. The motivation isn't where they are — it is the **distance** between where they are and where they want to go. (The Columbus bullet-hole story: Alec assumed a seller with bullet holes in her house and kids inside was in distress. She wasn't. She was just moving down the street and didn't care. Never assume the pain.)

### 4.1 The 10 Pain Categories

For each category, the rep should detect the category in what the seller says, then ask at least one **Impact question** (how is this affecting you?) and at least one **Socratic question** (what would change if you resolved it?). The AI should detect both the category and whether the rep responded with appropriate questions.

**1. Financial** — Debt, foreclosure, missed mortgage payments, tax delinquency.
- Impact: "What's going to happen if you continue to fall behind?" / "How is the stress of this affecting other areas of your life?"
- Socratic: "What options have you considered?" / "How do you see things improving if you sell now?"

**2. Emotional** — Stress, anxiety, attachment, overwhelm, grief.
- Impact: "How is holding onto this impacting your emotional well-being?" / "What emotional relief could you experience?"
- Socratic: "How would letting go affect your stress level?" / "What's holding you back from peace of mind?"

**3. Relationship** — Family disputes, divorce, inheritance fights, sibling conflict.
- Impact: "How are the disagreements affecting your relationships?" / "What impact could resolving this have on family dynamics?"
- Socratic: "What would happen if this continues unresolved?" / "How could selling bring relief or closure?"

**4. Physical** — Disrepair beyond their capacity, health/mobility issues, can't manage stairs.
- Impact: "What risks if the condition continues to decline?" / "How might your health or safety be affected?"
- Socratic: "What challenges in maintaining it?" / "How would your living situation improve if you sold?"

**5. Mental** — Overwhelmed by decisions, fatigue, can't think clearly about the property.
- Impact: "How much mental energy is this consuming?" / "What would peace of mind mean to have a clear path forward?"
- Socratic: "What's been the hardest part of deciding?" / "How do you feel about simplifying?"

**6. Time** — Lack of time to manage property, landlord exhaustion, tenant calls every other week.
- Impact: "How much of your time is this consuming?" / "What could you achieve if you weren't managing this?"
- Socratic: "How do you manage your time with these demands?" / "What other areas of your life could benefit?"

**7. Goal-Blocking** — Property prevents them from moving forward (job relocation, second mortgage, can't move on).
- Impact: "What opportunities are you missing?" / "How is this keeping you from your goals?"
- Socratic: "What goals have you put on hold?" / "How would your life change without this property?"

**8. Extreme Pleasure / Rewards Missed** — Stuck, missing positives, can't enjoy life because of the property.
- Impact: "What positive changes if you resolve this?" / "How much more could you enjoy life if this burden lifted?"
- Socratic: "What pleasures are you missing?" / "How do you envision life improving?"

**9. No Motivation** — Indifference, "I'll sell if the price is right," landlord with stable rental.
- Impact: "What's the cost of not taking action?" / "How might this situation worsen if no changes happen?"
- Socratic: "What would need to happen to make this important?" / "How do you see the future unfolding?"

**10. Toward-Pleasure-Only** — Retirement, moving to grandkids, Vegas weekend, downsizing for a dream lifestyle.
- Different tactic: get excited WITH them. "That's amazing. Where do the grandkids live? How old are they? How often do you see them now?" The pleasure motivation is just as actionable as pain — recap their pleasure back to them at the Offer Call.

### 4.2 The Picture Perfect

After identifying current situation and impact, ask: **"If you could paint me a picture-perfect scenario — you sell this house, and you start your next chapter — what exactly does that look like for you and your family?"** Their answer is the emotional anchor we recap back at the Offer Call.

### 4.3 The 7 Smokescreens / Resistance Patterns

The AI should detect these and verify the rep responded appropriately:

1. **"Not interested"** at the very start — defensive posture, hasn't given a chance
2. **Short answers / "yeah" / "mm-hmm"** — something is making them uncomfortable
3. **Hostility** — "How did you get my number? I have no for-sale sign"
4. **Rushing** — "I have 5 minutes, hurry up, just give me the price"
5. **Misleading / lying** — "Let me think about it" / "Let me talk to my mom" / "Let me pray on it" / "Let me think it over"
6. **"What's your offer?"** — pushing past discovery, trying to get a quick number to compare
7. **"I have a cash offer for $X higher"** — often a lie; verify by asking for closing date

### 4.4 Non-Committal Language

These triggers must NOT be accepted at face value. If the rep hears any of these, they must drill in:

- "Yeah, maybe"
- "Sure"
- "I think so"
- "It depends on the price"
- "We'll see"

The drill-in: "When you say 'I think so,' what exactly do you mean? What's holding you back from being confident?"

## 5. The Call Architecture — What the Rep Should Do

### 5.1 Intro (the 4 seconds)

**Script**: "Hey, this is [Name]. How are you today?"

- Smile when you say it. The seller can hear it on the phone.
- Warm, light energy. Not corporate, not rushed.
- For inbound leads: "I saw that you filled out information on our website about selling your property — is that still something you're considering?"
- For cold list: "I know this may be a little out of the blue, but I'm calling to see if you're interested in selling your property now or anytime in the near future."

**What the AI should check**: Did the rep introduce themselves by name? Did they sound warm? Did they avoid the corporate "May I speak to Mr. Smith?" pattern that telegraphs salesperson?

### 5.2 Set Expectations — TART

**Script**: "What I'd like to do is just take about 5 to 10 minutes to ask you some specific questions about the property — things like the age of the roof, the age of the furnace, things like that. After that, I'll talk with my partners and they'll let us know what the property qualifies for. All that I ask from you is that at the end of our conversation, you can make a confident yes or no decision. And if it's something you want to move forward with, great. But if not, it's no big deal — at least we gave it our best shot and you know what your options look like. Does that sound fair enough?"

The four components — all four must be present:

- **T**ime: "5 to 10 minutes"
- **A**genda: "specific questions about the property, then I'll talk with my partners"
- **R**esult: "we'll let you know what the property qualifies for, you can make a confident yes or no"
- Permission to say **No**: "if not, it's no big deal"

**What the AI should check**: Did the rep set all four components? Was permission-to-say-no offered explicitly? (Critical — this is what removes the pressure.)

### 5.3 The Urgency Qualifier

**Script**: "If we are able to come to an agreement on a fair price for the property, you are definitely ready to sell it, right?"

- This must come early — within the first 60 seconds after expectations.
- A clean YES means continue with the full Process.
- Anything other than YES → "I just want to make sure — is there something coming up that may be preventing you from being able to say yes or no?"
- If the seller is not distressed AND not urgent → SKIP to ballpark price immediately. Do not waste time on Process.

### 5.4 Motivation Discovery

**Open with the Situation Question**: "What's got you thinking about selling the property?"

- Open-ended. Never assume they're selling. (Avoid "Why are you selling?" — gets pushback.)
- Curious tonality, not interrogative.

**Then ask 2–3 Impact / Socratic questions** drilling into the pain category they reveal. Mirror their words. Use phrases like:

- "Tell me more about that."
- "When you say [their word], what do you mean?"
- "How does that make you feel?"
- "Would I be correct in saying this has caused you quite a bit of grief?"
- "Sorry you're in such a tough situation — would selling help alleviate some of that frustration?"
- "Would you say that's a big deal or small deal for you?"
- "I can sense some frustration around this property — is this something you're ready to have off your plate?"

**Explore for multiple motivations**: "Is there any other reason you may be selling the property?" — most sellers have layered motivations and won't volunteer all at once.

**Then ask the Picture Perfect**: "If you could paint me a picture-perfect scenario, you sell this house, and you start your next chapter — what does that look like?"

**What the AI should check**: Did the rep ask at least one Impact question per pain category mentioned? Did the rep mirror? Did the rep explore multiple motivations? Did the rep get a Picture Perfect answer? Was the tone empathetic (using "I can appreciate" and "I couldn't even imagine") rather than sympathetic ("I'm sorry" / "I understand")?

### 5.5 Property Condition Questions

The rep should cover all 16 standard items. If a seller pushes back with "you can find that online," the rep should answer: "Normally that's right, but public records sometimes have incorrect or outdated information, and they don't say anything about the age of the roof, the furnace, AC, things like that — which is why it's important I get that information so we can let you know a much more accurate number your property qualifies for. So when you say the windows were updated…"

The 16 items:

1. Confirm bed/bath count
2. Bathroom updates — vanity, shower (tile or vinyl), flooring
3. Kitchen updates — cabinet era (60s, 80s, 90s, modern), countertop type
4. Roof age + type (3-tab shingle / dimensional / metal / slate)
5. Windows — wooden, aluminum, or vinyl + age
6. Furnace and AC age
7. Hot water tank age
8. Electrical — "When you open the panel, do you have switches you flip, buttons you push, or open wires coming out?" (Don't ask "is it up to code" — everyone says yes.)
9. Plumbing type — cast iron, copper, galvanized steel, PVC, or PEX
10. Siding — asbestos shingle, stucco, brick, vinyl, aluminum
11. Garage — attached/detached, 1-car/2-car
12. Structural issues — foundation cracks, bowing walls, leaks
13. Flooring throughout — laminate, hardwood, LVP, peel-and-stick, carpet, sheet vinyl
14. Basement, crawl space, or slab? If basement: finished? Any flooding or sewage backups?
15. City water/sewer vs septic/well
16. Anything else the seller would fix themselves if they were staying?

**What the AI should check**: How many of the 16 items did the rep cover? Did they get specific (era of kitchen vs just "updated")? Did they handle pushback correctly?

### 5.6 Roadblocks — The 4 We Always Probe

This is where the rep extracts hidden value and prevents the deal from collapsing after verbal acceptance.

**Roadblock 1: Influencers** — "Would anybody be upset that you didn't speak with them first before making a decision to sell this property?"

- If NO → confirm: "So there's not a significant other, family member, or close friend you want to run this by?"
- If YES → DON'T STOP. You must determine three things about the influencer:
  - What do they want to see from this sale? (their motivation)
  - Do you both agree on timeline?
  - Do you both agree on price?
- If aligned on all three → "Do you feel you need them on the next call, or can you make a confident yes/no on their behalf?"
- If NOT aligned → schedule the influencer on the next call

Double and triple confirm influencers. This is the most common reason a deal collapses after verbal acceptance — the unannounced spouse, sibling, or attorney shows up at the last minute and kills it.

**Roadblock 2: Discomfort** — "Let's assume that you like our offer and you decide to move forward. What's the next thing you'd have to figure out, if anything at all?"

This is where the rep finds value-add opportunities. Sometimes a couple hundred dollars of help (a moving van, a storage unit, hooking up a trailer) translates to tens of thousands in perceived value to the seller. Double and triple confirm here too.

**Roadblock 3: Timeline** — **Word for word, this is not flexible:**

"We are flexible with a closing timeline and most people love that about working with us. Typically we close within 21 to 24 days. Some people love how quick we can close and others need more time. What time frame do you feel works best for you and your family?"

Three techniques embedded:
- "Most people love this" — social proof
- "21 to 24 days" — anchor (reality is 30–45 days; this makes their 30-day ask feel like a stretch)
- "What works best for you and your family" — they're now feeling like they got something

If the seller says 30 days, they feel like they stretched our criteria. That makes them less likely to stretch our criteria again on price.

**Roadblock 4: Money** — "**What do you feel is a fair price for the property?**"

The two F-words. **Feel** promotes emotion (not "think" — that's logic). **Fair** anchors lower than "ballpark" (which makes them hit the highest number out of the park).

If they refuse to give a number: "I can appreciate that. I know if I don't take a number back to fight with my partners, sometimes they set the price and it's not usually something you'd take. I want to see this as a win for you. Is there at least a range I can put in my back pocket and fight for?"

If still nothing: half-a-Zillow play — "Certainly you wouldn't take $100,000 for it, would you?"

**Common Enemy framing here is key**: the rep is the seller's advocate fighting with the partners. The partners are the bad guys. We're on the same side as the seller. "I want to see this as a win for YOU. They're going to buy a house regardless — yours or someone else's. I just want to see this work for you."

**What the AI should check**: Did the rep ask all 4 roadblocks? Did they double/triple confirm influencers? Did they use the exact Timeline language? Did they use "feel" and "fair" for the price ask? Did they use Common Enemy framing if there was price pushback?

### 5.7 Credibility Check

Before ending the Process Call, the rep should plant credibility:

"In the meantime, check us out on Facebook — Sell to Exodus — or you can go to selltoexodus.com [Jo to confirm URL]. I want you to feel comfortable with who you could potentially be working with. You'll see reviews from past sellers and some before-and-after photos. We're a locally owned and operated business right here in South Florida."

This solves the "who are you really?" doubt that grows in the 20–30 minute window between Process Call and Offer Call.

### 5.8 Set Expectations for the Offer Call

**Script**: "What I'm going to do is take 20 to 30 minutes to talk with my partners. They're going to let me know what the property qualifies for. I'm going to call you back. When I call you back, that usually takes about 5 minutes. I'll let you know exactly what it looks like to work with us and what the property qualifies for. At that time, all I'll ask is that you're ready to make a confident yes or no decision — and no is perfectly okay. With that being said, are there any questions for me? Are you going to be available at [time]?"

Lock in the specific time. The seller's brain leaves the call with a calendar commitment.

## 6. The 4 Objection Frameworks

Most objections are smokescreens. The goal is to get to the REAL objection and why it matters to the seller. Each of the 4 frameworks fits different turns in the conversation.

### Framework 1: Deflect & Redirect

**Use when**: Early-call questions about contact info, address, source, property details.

**Pattern**: Justify the concern → overcome with that justification → redirect with a question that returns to the sales process.

**Example — "How did you get my information?"**

❌ "We got it from XYZ list." (opens the door to more questions)

✅ "We received your information from our third-party marketing team, who focuses on homeowners in areas where we're actively looking to buy. Have you thought about selling your property, perhaps now or in the near future?"

**Example — "You can find that information online."**

✅ "Yes, you're right — for the most part, much of this is on the public records site. However, those records often only show basic details and can be outdated. They don't say anything about the age of the furnace or the roof, which is important for understanding their lifecycle. That's why I'd like to take 5 to 10 minutes to ask you more specific questions about the condition…"

### Framework 2: Go For No

**Use when**: The seller is fence-sitting, non-committal, or hedging. "Let me think about it." "Let me talk to my wife." "Let me pray on it." "I'm just exploring."

**Pattern**: Treat the hedge as a polite "no" — label it as such, with soft tone, and let them either confirm or correct.

**Script**: "Hey [Name], you've been very polite and I've enjoyed our conversation. With my experience, usually when someone tells me [their hedge], it's really just a polite way of saying no — and if it is, that's perfectly okay."

The 3 possible outcomes:

1. **They confirm the no** — saves you time. Don't chase ghosts for weeks.
2. **They correct you and state the real objection** — most common. "No, I want to sell, I just have to figure out [specific concern]." Now you have something to address.
3. **They correct you and want to move forward** — "No, actually I'm ready. Let's get the paperwork going." They closed themselves.

**Tone is critical** — soft, almost apologetic. "This 100% could be me, but typically when someone tells me they have to pray about it, I don't hear from them again. Is that what's going on here?" NOT confrontational. NOT accusatory.

**The principle**: Always be one step closer to no than the seller. Reverse psychology pulls them toward yes. Pressure pushes them away.

### Framework 3: Boxing Objections

**Use when**: The seller has given a hedge ("let me think about it") AND you've already executed Go-For-No, and they corrected you saying it's not a no. Now you need to find what's actually blocking the decision.

**Pattern**: Walk through the 4 categories of objection in a SPECIFIC ORDER — credibility first (low emotional stakes), price last (highest stakes). This order matters. Don't reorder.

**The 4 boxes in order**:

1. **The idea of working with me/our company** — "Was it me? Did I do something wrong? Was it the idea of working with our company?"
2. **The process** — "Was it the process? Do you have any questions about how this works?"
3. **The paperwork** (only if a PA has been presented) — "Was it the paperwork? Anything that didn't make sense?"
4. **The price** — "Was it the price?"

Plus: **Other offers/options** — "Are you considering other options? Are you talking to a realtor or other buyers?" If yes → pivot to the Multiple Offers framework.

**At the price box, switch to Same-Side Selling**:

"Okay. I really want to fight for you on this. Knowing where you're at — what's a number I can take back to them and fight for? No guarantees, but I really want to give this our best shot."

The rep is the seller's ally going up against the partners. The partners are the adversary. The seller is the rep's teammate.

### Framework 4: Multiple Offers

**Use when**: The seller is entertaining multiple offers from other buyers.

**First move — go for Premium Price**:

"Mr. Seller, is there a price you have in mind that if I can get approved for you, you'd award us the opportunity to assist you and not go with any other companies?"

If that doesn't work, three strategic options:

**Option 1 — Significantly High Offer** (when their asking is low): Offer $20K–$30K above their asking to stand out as generously favorable.

**Option 2 — Timing Game**: Schedule the walkthrough as the LAST potential buyer. Just before their deadline, reach out: "We're finalizing our numbers. Have all other offers been submitted? What would it take for our offer to be the most attractive?"

**Option 3 — Absolute Best Offer**: For rehabbers, have contractors quote. For wholesalers, get photos to VIP buyers only (don't widely market), bring 2–3 best buyers to the walkthrough, then make the highest offer that still allows the target profit.

## 7. The Offer Call Architecture

This is the most important call in the entire process. Practice it 2x more than the Process Call. You can stumble through a Process and still get the next call. You will never stumble through an Offer Call and get a contract.

### 7.1 Open

**Script**: "Hey [Name], this is [Rep]. Do you have about 5 minutes for a quick conversation?"

### 7.2 Pre-Frame

**Script**: "Great. So I spoke with the partners and they let me know what the property qualifies for. But before we talk about that, the partners want me to talk with you about what it looks like if you decide to work with us. Is that okay?"

This frames working with us BEFORE the number, so when the number lands, they're already evaluating it in the context of the value they get.

### 7.3 Confidence Check + Permission to Say No

**Script**: "At the end of this conversation, after I let you know what my partners say the property qualifies for, do you feel you'll have enough information to make a confident yes or no decision?"

"Again, if you decide you do not want to work with us, that's perfectly fine — you definitely won't hurt my feelings."

### 7.4 Walk the Full Process

**Script**: "First, I'll let you know what the property qualifies for. If you decide you'd like to move forward, I'll send over our written offer to your email and we'll both go through it together — that generally takes about 5 minutes or however long you need. Once I get your signature, we'll schedule a walkthrough to take photos to confirm the condition. After that, [TC's Name] our transactions coordinator will be in contact and will put you in touch with the title company. After title clears, closing will be scheduled. Any questions about that process?"

### 7.5 Recap Motivation — EMOTIONAL ONLY

This is the most-skipped step by rookie reps and the most important step on the call.

**Recap their motivation, their picture-perfect scenario, and their roadblocks** — all from what they told you on the Process Call. NEVER mention property condition. NEVER mention repairs. NEVER mention comparable sales.

**Script template**: "So about the property — [Name], I know you told me [their motivation, in their words], and a big thing for you is [their picture perfect]. I know you mentioned [their roadblock] — I actually fought for that with my partners and I was able to get them to approve [the value-add concession]."

The verbiage should keep them in the emotional state. Use words like "your next adventure," "peace of mind," "this win for you," "fresh start." Do NOT slip into logical price-input language like "based on comps in the area" or "given the repairs needed."

### 7.6 In-Pocket Math

**Script**: "And like I said before, this is what you'll walk away with in your pocket, minus any mortgage or liens and the property taxes — which is just a Florida thing. You don't have to pay any closing costs. You don't have to pay any realtor commissions. The property will be taken as-is, which means you don't have to do any cleaning and you don't have to do any repairs. You'll also be able to move on YOUR timeline."

### 7.7 Reveal the Number — With the Anchoring Trick

**Script**: "With that being said, I went back and spoke to my partners. I know you were really specific on [their asking price] and I fought, and I fought. After going back and forth, my partners originally came back at [a number BELOW your actual offer], but I let them know that wasn't going to get the deal done. I was able to get them to let me know your property qualifies for [your actual offer]."

**Even if your MAO is $260K, anchor the reveal at $255K or $258K and "fight" up to $260K.** The seller experiences your offer as a victory you won for them.

**For low-gap offers** (offering close to what they want): use full anchoring as above.

**For high-gap offers** (offering significantly below what they want): NEVER repeat their original asking. Anchoring their high number highlights the gap. Just say: "I know you were dealing with [X, Y, Z]. My partners let me know that the property qualifies for [your number]."

**For pocket-offer territory** (gap is too wide): see Section 8.4 below.

### 7.8 Silence

After the number, **stop talking**. If the seller reaches for air, stay silent. After 30–60 seconds: "How would you like to proceed?" Silence again.

We want the **first answer** that comes to their mind. Filling the silence lets them rationalize an objection. Letting it sit forces an honest reaction.

### 7.9 Handle the Response

**If yes** — gain commitment, set expectations for the Purchase Agreement call: "I'll take about 15 minutes to type the written offer, then I'll call you back. That call will take 5 minutes or however long you need. We'll review everything together. I just want to make sure you're 100% comfortable. Are you going to be available in 15 minutes with access to your email?"

**If no / hedge** — Go For No → Boxing Objections (Section 6). At the price box, use the **2:34 Hold Trick**:

"I really want to see this as a win for you. Let me go back and see what I can do — my partners are walking out of their office right now, give me 2 minutes. I'm going to put you on hold."

Mute. Wait 2 minutes and 34 seconds. Come back like you sprinted:

"Hey [Name], are you there? Okay — I've got some really good news. My partners said they had a property close for a little more than expected, so we have some premium funds available. I told them you were ready. They let me know that for today, they're willing to offer you [seller's asking price]."

You delivered their exact ask while staying the hero who fought to get it.

## 8. Special Plays

### 8.1 The Non-Committal Language Rescue (5 Steps)

When a seller is exploring without commitment, use this routine:

1. **Go For No** — "It seems to me that you're a little unsure about selling right now — exploring your options but not ready to make a decision anytime soon."

2. **Acknowledge & Align Emotionally** — "Totally get it — most of the people I talk to aren't sure what they want to do yet. They just want to understand their options before making any big decisions. Out of curiosity, what made you reach out today?"

3. **Unlock Motivation Through Curiosity** — "If you DID decide to sell, what would that allow you to do next? How long have you been thinking about making a change?"

4. **Bridge to Logic with Vision Framing** — "Got it. So if I could make the process easy, close on your timeline, and make sure you didn't have to deal with cleaning, repairs, or showings — would it make sense to at least look at some numbers together?"

5. **Remove Risk & Anchor with Soft Close** — "This doesn't lock you into anything — it just gives you information. That way, whether you decide to sell or not, you'll at least know what your options look like. Would it make sense for us to spend some time today to talk about the property, your goals with it, and the numbers?"

### 8.2 The Renegotiation Script

Used after a verbal/written agreement when something has come up (inspection finding, missed scope item, comp re-pull) and we need to lower the agreed price.

**Tonality is everything**. Structured pauses, ums, sighs, blame yourself, low slow tone. The goal is to make the seller feel BAD for the rep — like he's nervous, dreading this, on the seller's side, and trying to help.

**Script**:

"Hey [Name]... I've really just been dreading this call all day, but I know I need to give you an update. I just feel absolutely horrible... So as you know, our inspection came back, and... honestly, it's the reason for my call. I really wanted to make this work, and I probably should have just walked the property beforehand, but I felt we could get this one pushed through. When my partners reviewed the inspection feedback with me, they let me know there was [a crack in the foundation / a roof issue / etc.] that I didn't account for. I know you mentioned the foundation was great, so maybe it was even something you didn't know about. With that, and with [the other finding]... look, I know you were really trying to get this done so you could start your next chapter. And I know [their asking price] is what you wanted, and you wouldn't take a penny less. [SILENCE]"

The seller will break the silence: "Okay, what are you trying to tell me?"

"The partners let me know that after the walkthrough, to move forward, they would need to be at [new lower number]."

**The leverage**: by this point, the seller has mentally already moved out and spent the money. That makes them more flexible than they would have been at the Process Call. Use it.

### 8.3 Common Enemy Framing

The rep is the seller's advocate. "The partners" (or "financial partners," which is even more disposable — they can swap who the partners are) are the adversary.

Use it when:
- Seller pushes back on price → "I fought with them, here's what we got"
- Seller pushes back on terms → "Partners want this protection because they've been burned"
- Manager follow-up call → "Phil the Mean Manager" gave the bad news; the acquisition rep comes back as Good Cop: "Phil's the worst, disregard him, what do you need?"
- Renegotiation → "I told them I'm not bringing this back. They went and re-looked and found extra funds…"

The "financial partners" can be swapped at will: "I actually have other financial partners who only buy 2 properties a year because they rehab with their own hands, so they save money on repairs. Let me see if I can get them on this one."

### 8.4 Friend/Family Sale — Jump On Their Side

When a seller tells you they're going to sell to a friend, neighbor, family member: DO NOT object-handle. Get genuinely excited.

**Script**: "Dude, that's awesome. I wish everybody did that — passing assets down within the family. That's huge. Are they paying in cash? Have they been pre-approved? What are they going to do with their old property?"

Now you're poking holes — and you're staying #2 in line. When the friend deal falls apart (it usually does), you'll be the one they call.

### 8.5 The Pocket-Offer Move

When the gap is too wide (seller wants $300K, you can only offer $100K): DON'T give the offer.

**Script**: "Hey [Name], I'm not going to waste any of your time today, brother. I went and talked with my partners, and honestly, I just feel like they threw me out to the wolves to get eaten alive. I tried to support what you were looking for by doing the comps myself as well, and Steve, they just let me know what the property qualifies for, but we're just not going to be a good fit for what you're looking to achieve."

Tuck the offer. Follow up weekly. If they BEG more than twice to hear the number, give it. Once they reject it, trash the partners aggressively to maintain alignment: "Partners said $150K — that's ridiculous. I literally told them they're going to get me cussed out. I'm going to keep doing my own research because I think they're looking at something that's not correct. Can I call you back next week and check on you?"

### 8.6 The Homework Play

When the seller's asking price is far above market AND they won't budge: don't argue, don't undermine, don't give a low number that will offend them. Give them homework.

**Script**: "I'm always going to be honest with you. I don't know if we're going to be a good fit, but I don't want to give up. I want to see this as a win for you. So I'm going to go back, look at this more, do some more research. In the meantime, go look at houses in your area that have sold within the last 6 months — same square footage within 100 feet, similar age, similar condition. That'll give you an idea of what most companies will offer. I'll give you a call back next week."

The seller does the research, encounters market reality on their own, and comes back with realistic expectations. You're not the bad guy.

### 8.7 The Debt-to-Retire Discrepancy

When a seller says "I need $400K so I can retire debt-free," always ask "How much debt?"

If they say $304K, ask "What's the $96K for?" The gap between need and want is your negotiation room. Anchor to the GOAL (debt-free retirement), not their stated price.

**Script**: "I thought you wanted to accomplish [the goal]. If we can get you to [their actual need], does that accomplish what you really wanted?"

### 8.8 MLS-as-Reality-Check

When a seller insists on top MLS price:

**Script**: "Was the property you saw updated to 2026 conditions? Brand new roof, brand new appliances, fresh floors, everything? Okay — so that's top of market WITH a realtor, minus closing costs, minus realtor commissions, and including all the repairs to get there. If you want to go that route, I have my realtor right behind me — I can get him on the phone with you right now."

Most sellers will balk at the repairs. The realtor offer makes the as-is cash offer feel like the easier path.

### 8.9 Calling Out Lies — Gently

When the seller contradicts something they said earlier, or you sense they're lying: take the blame yourself.

**Script**: "I could have heard you wrong. I'm sorry — I thought I heard it this way. Maybe I misunderstood. Earlier we talked about [X], and now it sounds like [Y]. Help me understand what's changed?"

Direct, not accusative. The seller can either correct you or admit the change without losing face.

### 8.10 Tonality Calibration

- **Standard discovery**: warm, curious, conversational — like talking to a friend's grandparent
- **Empathy moment**: low, slow, soft — "I couldn't even imagine"
- **Hostile seller**: when they go UP, you go DOWN. Quieter, calmer, slower. Don't match energy.
- **Low offer reveal**: slow, deliberate, almost reluctant
- **Renegotiation**: dreading, blame-self, ums and pauses
- **Excited pleasure motivation**: warm enthusiasm, curiosity, get excited WITH them

The AI should detect tonality mismatch (e.g., aggressive tone with hostile seller, fast pace with emotional seller, logical/upbeat tone during renegotiation).

## 9. Empathy vs Sympathy

**Sympathy** = "I'm sorry you're going through that" / "I understand." You're outside their experience, observing.

**Empathy** = "I can appreciate that" / "I couldn't even imagine." You're alongside them, acknowledging without claiming to share their specific pain.

Never say "I understand" when a seller shares loss, grief, or hardship. You don't understand — you didn't know the loved one, you haven't lived their situation, you've been talking to them for 4 minutes. "I can appreciate" and "I couldn't even imagine" land authentically without claiming false common ground.

## 10. The AI Grading Rubric

For each recorded call, the AI should produce a structured analysis:

### 10.1 Call Identification
- Address / Seller name (from the deal record)
- Call type: Process / Offer / Purchase Agreement / Follow-up / Renegotiation
- Approximate length
- Outcome: contract signed / verbal agreement / next call scheduled / disqualified / ghost

### 10.2 UMBC Qualification Assessment
- **Urgency**: Yes / No / Not asked. If yes, supporting quote.
- **Motivation**: Distressed / Not Distressed / Unknown. Pain category(ies) identified — pulled from the 10 categories above.
- **Ballpark**: Number stated, vs Zillow estimate (provided by deal record), vs likely MAO.
- **Condition**: Livable / Not Livable / Unknown.
- **Verdict**: Qualified for Process? Qualified for Offer? Disqualified?

### 10.3 Section-by-Section Scoring

For each section, score: Strong / Adequate / Weak / Missed.

- Intro & 4-second impression
- Set Expectations — all 4 of TART
- Urgency Qualifier
- Motivation Discovery — pain categories explored, Impact + Socratic questions used, mirroring used, Picture Perfect captured
- Property Condition — number of 16 items covered, accuracy of answers
- Roadblocks — Influencers (single confirm vs double/triple), Discomfort, Timeline (verbatim?), Money (Feel/Fair?)
- Credibility Check
- Set Expectations for Next Call

For Offer Calls:
- Open + Pre-frame
- Confidence Check + Permission to Say No
- Full process walked
- Motivation recap (EMOTIONAL only?)
- In-Pocket math
- Reveal with Anchoring Trick
- Silence after number
- If pushback: Go-For-No → Boxing Objections → Same-Side Selling at price → 2:34 trick if needed

### 10.4 What the Rep Did Well

List 2–4 specific moments with quotes.

### 10.5 What the Rep Missed

List 2–4 specific gaps with quotes. For each, recommend the exact tactic that would have fit.

### 10.6 Pain Points the Seller Revealed

Plain-English bullets of every motivation, frustration, fear, or goal the seller mentioned — even ones the rep didn't follow up on. This is the AI's #1 value-add: surfacing pain the rep missed.

### 10.7 Next-Call Tactical Recommendations

Specific, actionable plays for the next call with THIS seller:

- "Recap [the specific pain category] back to them emotionally before reveal — use these words: [quote from their own words]"
- "Influencer [Name] was mentioned but not probed — confirm timeline + price alignment with them"
- "Seller said 'maybe' to urgency at minute 2:30 — execute Go-For-No to confirm"
- "Seller's asking price is $X above market — use the Homework Play, callback in 1 week"
- "Tonality recommendation: opening should be slower and warmer; seller seemed defensive in first 30 seconds"

### 10.8 Estimated Likelihood of Contract

Rough 0–100% gut-check based on:
- Strength of urgency
- Distress motivation present
- Ballpark/MAO gap
- Roadblocks addressed vs hidden
- Quality of rapport built

## 12. What This Methodology Is Not

This methodology is **not** about manipulating sellers into bad deals. Every play in this document has the same underlying purpose: surface what the seller actually needs and decide whether we can solve it. If we can't, we disqualify quickly and respectfully. If we can, we make the offer feel like the win it is.

The rep is the seller's representative inside our company. The partners (us) are the adversary the rep is fighting to get the seller what they need. That same-side framing isn't a sales trick — it's the actual truth: we want every contract we sign to be a clean win for both sides, because the alternative is a renegotiation, a backed-out deal, or a reputation hit when the seller tells their neighbor.
